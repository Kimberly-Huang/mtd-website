01 · Executive Summary

The value is proven. The bottleneck is conversion.

Omnichannel customers are worth 3.5× more per month and visit 3× more often than physical-only shoppers, a gap that holds steady across all 25 months. Yet penetration hasn't moved. This is not a retention problem; it is an activation problem, and it is solvable with the customers Swift already has on file.

3.5×
Omnichannel monthly spend (R$100.78) vs. physical-only (R$29.21), a structural, durable value gap.
6.4×
Priority 1 customers (high-value + prior online history) convert at 6.4× the base rate, the sharpest entry point.

Key Problems

  • Penetration is flat at 2.75–2.94% across 25 months, there is no organic drift toward the 6% target.
  • Only 1.33% of physical-only customers converted over 24 months; 41% went inactive instead. Churn is 31× more likely than conversion.
  • Online satisfaction (NPS +77.1) sits ~10 points below physical stores, pushing volume into a weaker channel risks brand damage.

Key Opportunities

  • ~69K Priority 1 customers already have online history and convert at 6.4× the base rate, a ready-made starting list.
  • 85%+ of physical-store customers are already reachable via Email or WhatsApp, no new acquisition spend needed.
  • 314 store-within-store locations have zero online-to-offline enablement, fixing this unlocks Click & Collect capacity.
02 · The Business Challenge

Omnichannel growth is a conversion challenge, not a retention one.

Of 2.57M physical-only customers, monthly gains of +4,261 conversions are almost entirely cancelled by −4,045 reversions, a near-zero net gain. Once customers convert, however, 97.5% stay. The problem sits squarely at the top of the funnel: activation.

03 · Structural Barriers

Two structural ceilings cap conversion today.

Before the strategy scales, two constraints must be understood: who Swift can actually see, and which stores can actually fulfil an online order.

Identification gap

Across two years, 70.88% of physical-store receipts carry a customer ID, but 29.12% (11.58M receipts) remain anonymous. Because each anonymous visit gets a brand-new ID, one shopper visiting five times appears as five strangers. These customers cannot be profiled, targeted, or converted through CRM.

Infrastructure gap

Among 798 active stores, 314 store-within-store (SWS) locations, Swift's single largest channel type, have zero O2O enablement. Every Click & Collect order requires O2O capability, so SWS is structurally excluded from online fulfilment today, even though it is the largest physical footprint.

05 · Exploratory Findings

Four signals that shape the strategy.

Satisfaction, product mix, seasonality, and reachability each point to the same conclusion, the levers for conversion already exist inside Swift's data.

How to reach physical customers

85%+ already have a digital touchpoint on file, no new acquisition needed

QR Code is the bridge

The highest-satisfaction touchpoint (+98.5 NPS). These customers already engage digitally in-store without buying online, the most natural first conversion target. Turning the QR scan into a CPF gateway can convert untrackable SWS footfall into CRM contacts.

Click & Collect beats delivery

Within online orders, Click & Collect scores +82.2 NPS vs. +76.0 for home delivery, a +6-point edge that makes it the priority fulfilment path and lead conversion offer.

Basket economics favour online

Online receipts average R$264 vs. R$126 physical (2.1×), with 1.6× higher margin, stable across the full panel. The conversion opportunity is structurally durable, not promotion-driven.

06 · The Strategy Framework

Who · What · When · How · Where

Five interrelated research questions form a sequential targeting engine, from scoring the right customers to piloting in the right region.

01
Who

Which physical-only customers to prioritise

A transparent priority score across Value, Engagement, Readiness & Momentum surfaces the customers most likely to convert.

H1: High-value, high-frequency customers with online readiness convert well above average.
02
What

Which categories unlock the first online purchase

Poultry, Pork, Seafood & Fruits/Veg out-penetrate beef online, everyday proteins are the low-friction entry offer.

H2: High online-adoption, high-frequency categories drive first online purchases.
03
When

When in the lifecycle to activate

Launch pre-December to ride the holiday peak; concentrate push nudges on Thu/Fri ahead of the Saturday churrasco peak (1.76× weekday volume).

H3: Activating at high-activity touchpoints beats random engagement.
04
How

Which channels & offers activate best

85%+ reachable via Email/WhatsApp; lead with Click & Collect (+6 NPS). Differentiate the message by priority band.

H4: Differentiated, characteristic-based activation beats one-size-fits-all.
05
Where

Where to pilot first

São Paulo, 93% of open O2O-capable stores, all LOJAS O2O-enabled. Geography is a deployment filter, not a scoring input.

H5: Regions with mature O2O and higher existing penetration make the best pilots.
✦
Overall Hypothesis

A transparent prioritisation model + targeted activation can lift penetration from ~3% to 6%.

The right message, in the right channel, per band.

São Paulo first

425 of 457 open O2O-capable stores (93%) are in São Paulo, all company-owned LOJAS fully O2O-enabled. The natural pilot geography; SWS (314 stores) is the structural ceiling to address next.

December seasonal peak

December averages R$89.6M vs. R$58.5M in June. Schedule Priority 1 high-effort activation 3–4 days before the peak, within Swift's 3–4 day campaign lead-time constraint.

The audience is ready now

85%+ reachable via Email (59%) or WhatsApp (26%). Path: fix online experience → enable SWS O2O → CRM to the 85% digital base → lead with Click & Collect.
